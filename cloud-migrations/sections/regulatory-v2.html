Key Regulations

  • nDSG — revised Swiss Data Protection Act (in force since 2023-09)
  • revFINMA Circular 2023/1 — operational risks & resilience; supersedes parts of 2018/3
  • GDPR — EU data protection (Regulation 2016/679)
  • DORA — EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (applicable since 2025-01)
  • EU Data Act — cloud-switching & portability rights (applicable since 2025-09)
Swiss-only audiences: emphasise nDSG and revFINMA 2023/1. Anyone with EU exposure: GDPR + DORA + EU Data Act all bite. DORA is the new big one for FINMA-regulated firms with EU customers or EU subsidiaries — overlaps heavily with the FINMA outsourcing framework on third-party risk and incident reporting. EU Data Act forces cloud providers to support customer-driven switching and forbids egress fees beyond cost-recovery from 2027.

The CLOUD Act question

US CLOUD Act

US providers can be compelled to disclose data, regardless of where it sits

FISA 702

Surveillance over non-US persons' data held by US providers

Provider responses

EU/Swiss sovereign regions · BYOK / HYOK / external KMS · confidential computing

This is THE objection regulators and security teams raise as soon as AWS/Azure/GCP enter the conversation. Address it head-on rather than hoping it doesn't come up. What to say: 1. US CLOUD Act (2018, Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act): a US court can compel a US-headquartered provider to hand over data they hold or control, regardless of where the data physically sits. So a Swiss customer's data in AWS Frankfurt is still in scope as long as AWS Inc. is the operator. 2. FISA Section 702: lets US intelligence surveil non-US persons' communications held by US "electronic communication service providers". Schrems II (CJEU C-311/18, 2020) struck down Privacy Shield over this exact issue. 3. Therefore "data residency" alone is NOT sovereignty. EU geography + US operator = US-court reach. Provider responses (the right-hand card): - Sovereign regions operated by EU/Swiss legal entities so the US-court compulsion path doesn't apply (AWS European Sovereign Cloud, Google + S3NS / Bleu, Swisscom Sovereign Cloud). - Customer-managed keys (BYOK / HYOK / external KMS): if the customer holds the keys, the provider technically cannot read the data even when compelled to surrender it. - Confidential computing: encryption-in-use via hardware enclaves (AWS Nitro, Azure Confidential, Intel SGX, AMD SEV-SNP). Sources to mention if asked: - US CLOUD Act: https://www.congress.gov/bill/115th-congress/house-bill/4943 - Schrems II: https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-311/18 - EDPB Recommendations 01/2020 on supplementary measures: https://edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/our-documents/recommendations/recommendations-012020-measures-supplement-transfer_en

What is Cloud Sovereignty?

Data

Subject to the laws of where it sits

Operational

No foreign admin access

Technical

Portability, open standards, no lock-in

"Digital sovereignty" is the umbrella term — strategic autonomy over digital infrastructure. The three cards are the concrete layers it breaks into. These are independent dials: a deployment can be data-sovereign (data in CH/EU) but not operationally sovereign (admin staff sit in the US), and vice versa. Microsoft, Google and Gartner all use roughly this three-layer framing. We dropped the original "Software Sovereignty" card here in favour of "Technical" — same idea, broader scope.

Do you actually need it?

Sovereignty follows data classification — not the other way around.
  • What data class do your assets fall into?
    • public internal confidential CID
  • Who is the regulator and what do they actually require?
  • Is encryption + customer-held keys enough — or is operator nationality in scope?
  • What cost / service-availability premium can you absorb?

Regulatory & Sovereignty Takeaways

  • It's not illegal to use the Cloud — Swiss banks and public sector already do
  • Classify data first, then pick a provider — not vice versa
  • Data residency alone is not sovereignty
  • Map regulators × data classes × providers in a matrix; review yearly
  • Key management is critical: BYOK / HYOK / external KMS
  • Sovereign ≠ secure — you still own the upper half of shared responsibility
  • Involve your lawyers early
